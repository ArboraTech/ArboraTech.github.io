Phase 1

Graphene Production

Summary

We plan to develop the infrastructure necessary to capture carbon dioxide in an efficient and sutainable manner, ensuring we can capture sufficient amounts of carbon dioxide gas to produce a significant amount of graphene. Next, we will develop the infrastructure necessary to produce graphene using this carbon dioxide, while we also partner with various businesses in the technology industry interested in purchasing our graphene.

How It Works

First, we plan on using direct carbon capture infrastructure to collect carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. This might involve developing our own carbon capture plants, or partnering with Climeworks to develop carbon capture plants. The plants work by passing ambient air containing carbon dioxide into the filters, where the CO2 is chemically bound to the filters. Next, once the filter is saturated with CO2, it is heated to around 100°C, releasing concentrated CO2. The CO2-free air is released back into the atmosphere.

Next, the concentrated CO2 gas can be processed to produce graphene. This is done in a one-step process that uses CO2 gas as carbon feedstock and copper-palladium alloys, with the reductive power of H2, inside an atmospheric pressure chemical vapour deposition (APCVD) reactor. The metallic subtrates are heated to 1000°C , after which the annealing process begins by passing through a mixture of argon and hydrogen gas for 30 minutes. The synthesis of graphene is then initated by passing a steady stream of CO2 into the reactor. This resulted in the formation of multi-layer graphene on the metallic substrates.

The cost and efficiency of this process are yet to be determined, and the graphene quality can still be improved by preventing the de-wetting of the copper palladium alloys. For more information on the process, read this paper.
